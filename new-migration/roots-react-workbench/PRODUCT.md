# Product

## Register

product

## Users

The primary users are numerical analysis students, instructors, and self-guided learners using Roots Workbench as a teaching calculator and answer workstation. They work during assignments, classroom demos, quiz preparation, and final project validation. They need to enter expressions quickly, select one numerical method, inspect finite-precision behavior, compare errors, and present enough evidence to trust the answer.

## Product Purpose

Roots Workbench helps users solve one-variable root-finding problems with Bisection, False Position, Fixed Point, Newton-Raphson, and Secant methods. Success means a student can move from expression entry to final root estimate, convergence status, iteration table, graph, and derivation evidence without leaving the work surface.

The product should feel like a focused calculator lab rather than a marketing page or generic dashboard. It exists to make the math immediate, inspectable, and presentable.

## Brand Personality

Modern, precise, and technically confident.

The voice is calm, direct, and instructional. It explains enough to build confidence without becoming chatty, decorative, or distracting. The visual tone can feel premium and Mac-like, but the math remains the focal point.

## Anti-references

Do not make the app feel like a corporate analytics dashboard, neon calculator skin, plain wireframe, worksheet PDF, or generic SaaS landing page. Avoid purple-blue AI gradients, decorative data visuals, heavy marketing copy, and any visual treatment that competes with formulas, graphs, result summaries, or iteration tables.

The current glass workbench direction is intentional, but it should read as a focused operating environment, not decorative blur for its own sake.

## Design Principles

1. Make the math feel immediate: input, method, result, graph, and iteration evidence should feel visually connected.
2. Keep calculator workflows fast: controls should be compact, keyboard-friendly, responsive, and efficient for repeated classroom use.
3. Treat the Result Console as the anchor: the final root estimate and convergence status should be the first thing users can trust.
4. Use atmosphere to frame the work, not to decorate it: the Mac-style glass surface should support focus and hierarchy.
5. Preserve trust and accessibility: maintain readable contrast, visible focus states, responsive tables, reduced-motion safety, and predictable controls.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA for contrast, focus visibility, semantic structure, and keyboard access. Touch targets should be practical on mobile, especially for symbol entry, evidence tabs, toolbar buttons, and compact panel actions. Motion should be restrained and state-driven, with no decorative choreography that slows a student down.
