## Design Context

### Users
The primary users are numerical analysis students, instructors, and self-guided learners using the app as a teaching calculator. They need to enter expressions quickly, compare exact and finite-precision results, inspect errors, and understand iterative methods through visible computation steps. Their context is often assignment work, classroom demos, checking solutions, or learning why a numerical method behaves the way it does.

### Brand Personality
The interface should feel modern, precise, beautiful, and technically confident. It should combine the clarity and restraint of Apple-style product design with the approachable mathematical fluency of online graphing calculators like Desmos. The product voice should be calm, direct, and instructional: it explains enough to build confidence without becoming chatty or decorative.

### Aesthetic Direction
Use a polished modern calculator-lab direction: clean light surfaces, excellent spacing, crisp typography, responsive layouts, refined controls, readable tables, and graph/result areas that feel immediate and trustworthy. Favor a light-mode-first experience with a practical dark mode. Visual details should feel intentional and premium, but the math must remain the focal point.

Reference direction: Apple for restraint, hierarchy, spacing, and perceived quality; Desmos for fast mathematical interaction, approachable controls, and a tool that feels powerful without feeling heavy.

Avoid overly playful, neon, corporate-dashboard, or plain wireframe aesthetics. Do not let decoration compete with formulas, graphs, iteration tables, or result summaries.

### Design Principles
1. Make the math feel immediate: inputs, outputs, graphs, error values, and iteration steps should be easy to scan and visually connected.
2. Prioritize beautiful clarity: use spacing, typography, alignment, and contrast to make the interface feel premium without adding visual noise.
3. Keep calculator workflows fast: controls should be compact, keyboard-friendly, responsive, and efficient for repeated classroom or assignment use.
4. Teach through the interface: short labels, states, and result explanations should help users understand numerical behavior without interrupting the workflow.
5. Preserve trust and accessibility: maintain high contrast, durable focus states, responsive tables, reduced-motion support, and predictable behavior across devices.
